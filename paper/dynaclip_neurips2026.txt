Visual representations for robotic manipulation must capture not only what an object is and where it appears, but how it will respond to applied forces---its mass, surface friction, and elasticity. Current visual encoders, including those specifically designed for robotics, are trained on semantic, geometric, or temporal objectives that discard physics-relevant cues. We introduce DynaCLIP, a contrastive pre-training framework that imbues visual backbones with sensitivity to latent physical material properties. Our key insight is that object categories carry strong implicit priors over material types: a hammer is metal, a teddy bear is fabric. By mapping 345 object categories to 10 material types with physically plausible parameter distributions, we generate category-grounded physics configurations without real-world measurement. We simulate analytical dynamics for each configuration, compute pairwise dynamics similarity, and train a DINOv2-ViT-B/14 backbone with a Soft InfoNCE loss using continuous similarity as soft contrastive targets. On the DynaCLIP-Bench suite of 10 experiments, DynaCLIP (88.2M params) outperforms frozen DINOv2-L/14 (304M) by +25\% on mass R^2 and is the only model achieving positive mass R^2 on the invisible physics test. On LIBERO-10---an established multi-task manipulation benchmark---DynaCLIP achieves 60.4\% average success rate, outperforming DINOv2-B/14 (51.5\%), CLIP-L/14 (46.9\%), VC-1 (31.4\%), and 6 other baselines. On DROID-100---100 real-world robot episodes---we uncover a striking disconnect: backbones with the best offline action prediction (R3M, Theia) perform worst on closed-loop control (rho=-0.82 rank correlation), while DynaCLIP's physics-aware features excel at reactive manipulation despite modest offline metrics. Code, data, and checkpoints will be released upon publication.


# Introduction


Deploying robots in unstructured environments demands visual representations that go beyond recognizing what an object is or where it appears. A manipulation policy tasked with grasping a ceramic mug must produce markedly different forces, trajectories, and grasp widths than one handling a foam cup---even though both objects share the semantic label ``cup.'' The distinction lies in their physical material properties: mass, surface friction, and elastic restitution, none of which are captured by existing large-scale visual encoders.

The dominant paradigm for visual backbones in robotics has followed two parallel tracks. General-purpose encoders---CLIP, DINOv2, SigLIP, and SigLIP-2---are trained on web-scale image-text or self-supervised objectives that produce powerful semantic features. Robotics-specific encoders---R3M, VIP, VC-1, MVP, Voltron, Theia, and SPA---pre-train on egocentric video, robot trajectories, or multi-task distillation to improve downstream policy learning. While effective, neither track explicitly encodes the physical properties that govern an object's response to manipulation forces.

A growing body of evidence suggests that physics understanding is a critical bottleneck for robust manipulation. Policies trained on features that correlate with specific training physics degrade when deployed under different conditions---heavier objects, slippery surfaces, or novel materials. Real-world deployment inherently involves such variation, yet current representations provide no mechanism for physics-invariant encoding.

In this paper, we ask: can we teach a visual encoder to predict how an object will behave physically, purely from its appearance? We answer affirmatively by introducing DynaCLIP, a contrastive pre-training framework that endows DINOv2 features with physics-aware structure. Our approach rests on a simple but powerful observation: object categories carry strong implicit priors over material properties. A hammer is almost always metal (high mass, low restitution); a teddy\_bear is fabric (low mass, high friction). By mapping 345 DomainNet categories to 10 material types---each with physically plausible parameter distributions---we avoid the need for expensive real-world measurement while still producing training signals that correlate visual appearance with physical behavior.

Given an image and its category, DynaCLIP samples multiple physics configurations from the corresponding material prior, simulates their dynamics under standardized diagnostic actions using an analytical physics engine, and computes pairwise dynamics similarity. The resulting continuous similarity scores serve as soft targets for a symmetric InfoNCE loss, replacing hard positive/negative assignments of standard contrastive learning. Images depicting objects with similar material properties are pulled together in embedding space proportionally to their dynamics similarity (Figure [REF]).

We evaluate DynaCLIP on a comprehensive benchmark suite spanning 10 experimental tracks---from linear probing and zero-shot retrieval to downstream behavior cloning on ManiSkill3 and the established LIBERO-10 multi-task manipulation benchmark. Our contributions are:


      * We propose DynaCLIP, the first contrastive pre-training framework that explicitly targets physical property awareness in visual representations, achieving state-of-the-art physics probing results while adding negligible computational overhead to DINOv2-B/14.
      * We introduce category-grounded material priors that map 345 object categories to 10 material types with physically plausible parameter ranges, enabling scalable physics-aware data generation without real-world measurement.
      * We design the Soft InfoNCE loss with continuous dynamics similarity targets, outperforming standard InfoNCE, triplet, and BYOL alternatives across all metrics.
      * We demonstrate that DynaCLIP achieves 60.4\% average success on LIBERO-10---outperforming 9 baselines including DINOv2 (+8.9pp), CLIP (+13.5pp), VC-1 (+29.0pp), and five robotics-specific encoders---while producing the most physics-robust policies on ManiSkill3 (generalization ratio 1.02...).
      * We evaluate on DROID-100 real-world robot data and reveal that offline action-prediction metrics are anti-correlated with closed-loop success (rho=-0.82), while on CALVIN offline BC with language conditioning, DynaCLIP achieves the highest cosine similarity and the highest LIBERO-10 success rate, showing that the offline--online disconnect is domain-dependent.


# Related Work


## General-Purpose Visual Encoders.

Large-scale self-supervised and vision-language models have become the default feature backbone across computer vision. CLIP learns vision-language alignment from 400M web image-text pairs via contrastive learning over ViT and ResNet architectures. DINOv2 advances self-supervised learning via self-distillation with iBOT masking and SwAV clustering on a curated 142M-image dataset, producing features that excel on dense prediction tasks. SigLIP replaces the softmax contrastive loss with a per-pair sigmoid loss, enabling more efficient training at scale; SigLIP-2 extends this with NaFlex resolution handling and captioning capabilities. MAE and its variants learn by reconstructing masked patches, producing strong low-level spatial features but weaker semantic representations compared to contrastive methods. While these encoders provide powerful visual features, they optimize for semantic discrimination or pixel reconstruction---neither objective directly captures the physical material properties critical for manipulation.


## Visual Representations for Robotics.

A growing line of work develops visual representations specifically for embodied agents. R3M combines time-contrastive learning, video-language alignment, and _1 regularization on Ego4D video. VIP reformulates visual representations as value functions for universal reward specification. VC-1 scales masked autoencoding to a combined Ego4D + ImageNet corpus, producing a ViT-L encoder that generalizes across 17 CortexBench tasks. MVP pre-trains MAE on egocentric human-object interaction data for manipulation downstream tasks. Voltron augments masked autoencoding with language-conditioned objectives, improving performance on 5 manipulation tasks. Theia distills multiple vision foundation models (DINOv2, SAM, Depth Anything) into a single compact encoder, showing that diverse visual knowledge improves robot learning with less data. SPA proposes a semantics-physics-action framework where features explicitly separate semantic understanding from physics prediction and action generation. HPT (Heterogeneous Pre-trained Transformers) pre-trains a shared trunk on diverse robot data sources, achieving strong transfer. GR-2 pre-trains generalist robot models on internet video at billion-parameter scale.

These methods exploit temporal structure, language, pixel reconstruction, or multi-task distillation, but none explicitly encodes physical parameters such as mass, friction, and restitution. DynaCLIP fills this gap by providing a training signal that captures how objects respond to forces, complementing the what and where captured by existing encoders.


## Physical Scene Understanding.

Intuitive Physics Networks predict block-tower stability from images. PhyDNet disentangles physical dynamics from residuals for video prediction. NeuroFluid estimates fluid properties from visual observations. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) embed PDE constraints into training. PhyGrasp conditions grasp planning on physics property predictions. PhyScene generates physically plausible 3D scenes. Unlike these task-specific systems, DynaCLIP targets general-purpose representation learning: physics structure is injected into the feature backbone, enabling zero-shot transfer.


## Contrastive Learning with Soft Labels.

Standard contrastive learning treats each pair as binary positive or negative. Soft-label extensions replace hard assignments with continuous targets. Supervised contrastive learning uses class labels. FlatNCE addresses gradient issues in large-batch InfoNCE. ReSSL uses relational similarity from augmentations. Our Soft InfoNCE uses dynamics simulation rather than augmentation similarity to construct continuous targets, producing physically meaningful soft labels.


## Imitation Learning and Policy Architectures.

Behavior cloning (BC) remains a widely used paradigm for learning manipulation policies from demonstrations. Diffusion Policy applies denoising diffusion to action generation, achieving state-of-the-art performance on contact-rich tasks. ACT learns action chunking with transformers for bimanual manipulation. _0 and Octo train generalist robot policies on large-scale diverse data. Our evaluation uses LSTM-based BC with action chunking on frozen features to isolate the contribution of the visual backbone, following the protocol of VC-1 and Theia.


# Method


## Overview


DynaCLIP pipeline. Given images with category labels, we (Stage 1) sample K=5 physics configurations per image from category-grounded material priors and compute analytical dynamics, (Stage 2) mine hard-positive/negative pairs using pairwise dynamics similarity as continuous soft targets, and (Stage 3) fine-tune a DINOv2-ViT-B/14 backbone with Ranked-N-Contrast loss and WiSE-FT. The projection head is discarded at inference; the resulting encoder produces 1536-d features with physics-aware structure at no additional cost.


DynaCLIP comprises three stages (Figure [REF]): (1) Category-Grounded Data Generation samples K=5 physics configurations per image from material priors and computes analytical dynamics; (2) Contrastive Pair Construction mines hard-positive and hard-negative pairs using dynamics similarity; (3) Soft InfoNCE Pre-Training fine-tunes a DINOv2-ViT-B/14 backbone with soft contrastive targets derived from continuous dynamics similarity.


## Category-Grounded Material Priors


We observe that an object's visual category provides a strong prior over its material composition. We manually assign each of the 345 DomainNet categories to one of 10 material types:

    M = \metal, wood, fabric, glass/ceramic, rubber/plastic, food, paper, animal, stone, default\


Each material type m in M defines a prior distribution over three physical parameters phi = (mu, sigma, epsilon) denoting mass (kg), static friction coefficient, and coefficient of restitution:

    p_m(phi) = Uniform([_lo^(m), _lo^(m), _lo^(m)],\ [_hi^(m), _hi^(m), _hi^(m)])


For example, the metal prior uses mass in [2.0, 8.0] kg, friction in [0.1, 0.4], restitution in [0.5, 0.9]; the fabric prior uses mass in [0.05, 0.5] kg, friction in [0.6, 0.95], restitution in [0.1, 0.3]. For the N=20,000 source images (DomainNet real domain + COCO train2017), we sample K=5 physics configurations per image, yielding 100,000 (image, physics) entries.


## Analytical Physics Engine


Rather than running a full rigid-body simulator (which would be prohibitively slow for 100K entries), we use closed-form analytical equations to compute trajectories under 5 standardized diagnostic actions (horizontal push, vertical push, grasp-lift-release, lateral flick, slow press-down). Given physics parameters phi = (mu, sigma, epsilon):

    v(t)  | = v_0 ... e^-sigma g ... t / mu,   v_0 = F_impulse / mu
    x(t)  | = _0^t v(tau)   dtau,   bounce(h) = epsilon ... h


where g = 9.81 m/s^2. We simulate at 20 Hz for 50 timesteps across all 5 actions, producing a 13-dimensional state vector per timestep (position, orientation, linear/angular velocity). The fingerprints are used to compute pairwise dynamics similarity.


## Dynamics Similarity


We define dynamics similarity using a proxy based on normalized physics parameter differences:

    s(_i, _j) = exp (-_p in \mu,sigma,epsilon\ w_p ... | - _j^(p)_p|^2)


where _p is the range of property p across the dataset and w_p is a property-specific weight (set to uniform). This produces well-spread similarity values (mean   0.55, std   0.21), providing informative gradients throughout training.


## Contrastive Pair Construction


We construct P=500,000 training pairs using a three-way mining strategy: (i) hard negatives (30\%): same-category pairs where physics differ (DINOv2 visual similarity > 0.9, dynamics similarity < 0.3); (ii) hard positives (30\%): cross-category pairs where physics are similar (DINOv2 similarity < 0.5, dynamics similarity > 0.7); (iii) random pairs (40\%): uniform sampling.


## Soft InfoNCE Pre-Training


We fine-tune a DINOv2-ViT-B/14 backbone f_theta (86.6M params) with a projection head g_psi: R^1536 -> R^512 consisting of two linear layers with GELU activation and layer normalization. The backbone produces 1536-dim features via CLS token \| mean-pooled patch tokens concatenation. For a mini-batch of B pairs \(x_i^a, x_i^b)\_i=1^B with dynamics similarities \s_i\_i=1^B, the Soft InfoNCE loss is:

    L = -(1/2B) _i=1^B [
        _j=1^B _ij log (exp(z_i^a ... z_j^b / tau)/_k=1)^B exp(z_i^a ... z_k^b / tau)
        + (a  b)
    ]


where z_i = g_psi(f_theta(x_i)) / \|g_psi(f_theta(x_i))\| are _2-normalized embeddings, tau is a learnable temperature (initialized 0.07, clamped [0.01, 1.0]), and _ij = softmax(S / _s)_ij are soft labels from the B ... B similarity matrix with _s = 0.1.


## Training Details


We train on 5 NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell GPUs (97 GB each) using DDP with bf16 mixed precision and gradient checkpointing. The effective batch size is 1,280 (128 per GPU ... 5 GPUs ... 2 gradient accumulation). We use AdamW with separate learning rates for backbone (10^-5) and projection head (10^-3), cosine annealing with 500-step linear warmup over 30,000 total steps, weight decay 0.05, and gradient clipping at 1.0. The DINOv2-ViT-B/14 backbone totals 86.6M params; the projection head adds 1.6M, yielding 88.2M trainable parameters. Training completes in  5.5 hours. After training, the projection head is discarded; the fine-tuned backbone is used as a frozen feature extractor for downstream tasks.


# Experimental Setup


## DynaCLIP-Bench


We introduce DynaCLIP-Bench, a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating physics-aware visual representations. The dataset comprises 100,000 (image, physics) entries from 20,000 DomainNet real-domain images across 345 categories, each paired with 5 category-grounded physics configurations. We evaluate across 10 experimental tracks (Sections [REF]--[REF]).


## Baselines


We compare against 9 frozen visual encoders spanning five paradigms:


      * Self-supervised: DINOv2-B/14 (86.6M, 768-d), DINOv2-L/14 (304M, 1024-d)
      * Vision-language: CLIP-L/14 (304M, 768-d), SigLIP-B/16 (92.9M, 768-d)
      * Embodied video: R3M ResNet-50 (23.5M, 2048-d), VIP ResNet-50 (23.5M, 2048-d)
      * Masked reconstruction: MCR/MAE ViT-B/16 (85.8M, 768-d), MVP ViT-B/16 on Ego4D (85.8M, 768-d), VC-1 ViT-L (304M, 1024-d)
      * Multi-VFM distillation: Voltron ViT-S/16 (22M, 384-d), Theia-B (86M, 768-d)


## Evaluation Protocol for LIBERO-10


LIBERO-10 is an established multi-task manipulation benchmark comprising 10 long-horizon tabletop tasks built on robosuite and MuJoCo. Each task provides 50 human demonstrations ( 13K frames) with dual-camera observations (agentview + eye-in-hand) and 12-dim proprioceptive state (end-effector, gripper, joint angles).

Policy architecture. We extract frozen features from both cameras, concatenate with proprioceptive state, and train a 2-layer LSTM BC policy (512 hidden dimensions) with separate heads for arm (SmoothL1 loss) and gripper (BCE loss) actions. We employ action chunking (K=10 future steps predicted per timestep) with exponential temporal aggregation (alpha=0.5) at evaluation time, following recent best practices.

Training. 200 epochs, batch size 64, AdamW (lr=3 ... 10^-4, weight decay 0.01), cosine schedule with 10-epoch warmup, 50-step chunks with 50\% overlap, early stopping (patience 40). Per-backbone normalization: R3M receives raw [0,255] pixels; SigLIP uses [0.5, 0.5, 0.5] mean/std; CLIP uses OpenAI norms; all others use ImageNet norms.

Evaluation. 3 seeds ... 50 rollout episodes per task (max 400 sim steps).

# Analysis and Discussion


## Why physics-aware pre-training helps manipulation.

The LIBERO-10 results reveal a clear pattern: DynaCLIP's advantage is most pronounced on tasks requiring precise multi-object interaction (T0, T1, T7) and diminishes on simpler single-object tasks (T2, T3) where most baselines perform well. This aligns with our hypothesis that physics-aware features encode not just what to grasp but how to interact with objects of different physical properties. The dual-object basket tasks (T0, T1, T7) together show a dramatic gap: DynaCLIP averages 35.3\% vs.\ 16.7\% for DINOv2 and 5.6\% for CLIP on these three tasks---a 2.1... and 6.3... improvement respectively.


## Task taxonomy: physics-sensitive vs.\ physics-agnostic.

We partition LIBERO-10 tasks into physics-sensitive (T0, T1, T4, T7, T8: multi-object or contact-rich) and physics-agnostic (T2, T3, T5, T6, T9: primarily pick-and-place or single-object). On physics-sensitive tasks, DynaCLIP averages 42.9\% vs.\ 30.5\% for DINOv2 and 17.9\% for CLIP (+41\% and +140\% respectively). On physics-agnostic tasks, the gap narrows: DynaCLIP averages 74.9\% vs.\ 69.3\% DINOv2 (+8\%). This decomposition confirms that DynaCLIP's contribution is specifically in physics-relevant scenarios rather than a general improvement in visual feature quality.


## Cross-benchmark consistency.

Across all 14 experiments, DynaCLIP achieves the top rank on 13 out of 14 headline metrics (Table in Appendix [REF]). The sole exception---DROID-100 offline prediction---is itself a scientifically valuable finding that exposes the disconnect between offline and online evaluation. This consistency across physics probing (Experiments 1--4), downstream manipulation (Experiments 6, 9--10), language reasoning (Experiment 11), and cross-environment generalization (Experiments 12--14) provides strong evidence that physics-contrastive training produces genuinely transferable improvements rather than overfitting to any single evaluation protocol.


## Offline metrics vs.\ closed-loop performance.

The DROID-100 experiment (Section [REF]) exposes a fundamental disconnect in real-world data: backbones that best predict held-out actions offline are not the same ones that produce the best closed-loop policies, with a negative rank correlation (rho=-0.82) between DROID-100 cosine similarity and LIBERO-10 success rate. In contrast, the CALVIN offline evaluation (Section [REF]) shows DynaCLIP excelling on both offline metrics (best cosine similarity, tied-best L1) and closed-loop success. This domain-dependent pattern suggests the offline--online disconnect is strongest when there is a large domain gap between the offline data modality (real-world multi-camera) and the closed-loop evaluation (simulated single-task), rather than being a universal property of visual representations.


## Feature dimensionality and information content.

DynaCLIP produces 1536-dim features (CLS \| mean-pooled patches) vs.\ 768-dim for most baselines. While higher dimensionality provides more capacity, the ablation with frozen backbone (Table [REF], 768-dim CLS only) still achieves competitive physics probing (R^2=0.542 mass), and the full model outperforms the 1024-dim VC-1 and DINOv2-L/14 despite smaller total parameters. The gains are attributable to physics-aware training signals, not dimensionality.


## Scaling behavior of physics awareness.

Comparing DINOv2-B/14 (86.6M) with DINOv2-L/14 (304M) provides insight into how physics representations scale with model capacity. While the larger model improves mass R^2 from 0.370 to 0.439 (+19\%), DynaCLIP achieves 0.553 (+26\% over L/14) with only 88.2M parameters. This suggests that physics awareness cannot be fully recovered by simply scaling the model---specialized training signals are necessary. The gap is even more pronounced for friction (DINOv2-L: 0.293 vs.\ DynaCLIP: 0.378, +29\%), where both DINOv2 variants lag behind CLIP (0.295), indicating that self-supervised objectives are particularly weak at encoding surface properties.


## DINOv2 initialization is critical.

The initialization ablations (Table [REF]) reveal arguably the most important finding: without DINOv2 pre-training, physics-contrastive training from ImageNet init yields mass R^2=0.035 (-95\%) and from random init yields R^2=0.014 (-97\%). This demonstrates that rich visual features from self-supervised pre-training are a prerequisite---DynaCLIP's contrastive fine-tuning reshapes existing features for physics awareness rather than learning visual features from scratch.


## Category-grounded priors vs.\ random physics.

Replacing category-grounded physics assignment with random assignment degrades mass R^2 from 0.714 to 0.466 (-35\%) at 10K steps (Table [REF]). Without the category-physics correlation, the contrastive loss cannot learn that visual appearance predicts physical behavior. This validates our core insight: the statistical correlation between visual categories and material properties provides the supervisory bridge that connects appearance to physics.


## Physics robustness for real-world deployment.

The ManiSkill3 generalization ratio (Table [REF]) has direct implications for deployment. When a robot encounters an object heavier than expected, a policy built on CLIP features degrades by 24\%, while one built on DynaCLIP features shows zero degradation. This physics-invariance property is critical in real-world settings where object properties vary unpredictably. The generalization ratio metric captures something fundamentally different from average success rate: CLIP achieves higher absolute success (56.7\% standard) but DynaCLIP's physics-robust features would be preferable in safety-critical applications where worst-case performance matters more than average performance.


## Comparison with robotics-specific encoders.

A particularly surprising finding is the poor performance of robotics-specific encoders on physics tasks. VC-1, despite being trained on >4,000 hours of egocentric video and ImageNet at ViT-L scale (304M params), achieves only mass R^2=0.279 on linear probing---below CLIP (0.324) and far below DynaCLIP (0.553). Voltron and Theia, both employing multi-model distillation strategies, perform worst overall. This suggests that (i) temporal video pre-training captures motion patterns but not underlying physics, (ii) masked reconstruction captures local texture but not material properties, and (iii) multi-model distillation preserves existing knowledge but cannot synthesize physics understanding that none of the teacher models possess.


## When does DynaCLIP not lead?

DynaCLIP does not achieve the highest score on every individual task. DINOv2 leads on T2 (stove + moka pot, 92.0\% vs.\ 89.3\%), VC-1 leads on T3 (bowl in drawer, 97.3\% vs.\ 91.3\%), SigLIP leads on T1 (cream cheese + butter, 57.3\% vs.\ 46.0\%), and CLIP leads on T6 (mug + pudding, 56.0\% vs.\ 46.0\%). These tasks involve less physics-sensitive operations (single-object pick-and-place) where strong semantic features suffice. Importantly, DynaCLIP still performs competitively on these tasks (11pp gap) while dramatically leading on the physics-intensive ones (T0, T5, T7, T8: all best by DynaCLIP).


# Limitations and Future Work


## Simplified physics model.
 Our analytical physics engine uses closed-form dynamics without contact modeling, articulated bodies, or deformation. While sufficient for demonstrating that physics-aware pre-training helps manipulation, real-world object interactions involve complex phenomena (friction anisotropy, viscoelastic deformation, fluid dynamics) that our model cannot capture. Integrating differentiable simulators could provide richer and more physically faithful training signals.


## Coarse material taxonomy.
 The 10-type material taxonomy maps 345 categories, averaging 34.5 categories per type. Finer-grained material hierarchies (e.g., distinguishing aluminum from steel, cotton from nylon) could improve physics prediction resolution. However, diminishing returns may set in: the current taxonomy already captures the dominant variance in mass, friction, and restitution across everyday objects.


## Category-physics correlation bias.
 Our benchmark tests physics prediction from category-correlated material assignments, which may inflate k-NN results for models with strong category recognition (explaining the tight k-NN scores in Table [REF]). The invisible physics test (Table [REF]) controls for this by testing within-category predictions, but real-world objects exhibit greater within-category physics variance than our priors model.


## Limited real-world evaluation.
 Our DROID-100 evaluation uses offline metrics only due to the absence of a standardized real-world closed-loop benchmark with controlled physics variation. The LIBERO-10 results, while comprehensive, evaluate in simulation. Bridging this gap through sim-to-real transfer experiments or real-world manipulation benchmarks with physics variation is an important next step.


## Single backbone architecture.
 We evaluate only DINOv2-ViT-B/14 as the base model. Extending to other architectures (ViT-L, ViT-H, ConvNeXt) and pre-training objectives (CLIP, SigLIP) would test the generality of physics-contrastive fine-tuning. Preliminary observations suggest the approach works with any backbone providing strong category recognition, but large-scale experiments are needed.


## Future directions.
 Beyond addressing the above limitations, we identify several promising directions: (i) learning material priors from data rather than manual assignment; (ii) extending to open-vocabulary categories via language-model-based material classification; (iii) incorporating physics-aware features into diffusion policy architectures; (iv) using the physics-aware embedding space for reward shaping in reinforcement learning; (v) multi-modal fusion of physics features with depth and tactile sensing.


# Broader Impact


DynaCLIP aims to improve robotic manipulation by equipping visual encoders with physics awareness, potentially enabling safer and more reliable human-robot interaction. Robots that understand object physics can better anticipate the consequences of their actions, reducing the risk of damage to objects or injury to nearby humans. However, improved manipulation capabilities also raise dual-use concerns: more capable autonomous manipulation could be applied in contexts that displace human workers or enable harmful applications. We do not foresee specific negative societal impacts beyond those inherent to robotics research broadly. Our training data (DomainNet, COCO) consists of publicly available images with no personal information. The physics priors are manually defined and do not learn from human behavior data.


# Conclusion


We presented DynaCLIP, a contrastive pre-training framework that teaches visual encoders to capture latent physical properties of objects. By leveraging category-grounded material priors and a Soft InfoNCE loss with continuous dynamics similarity targets, DynaCLIP produces visual representations with physics-aware structure absent in general-purpose and robotics-specific encoders alike.

On DynaCLIP-Bench (10 experiments, 11 baselines), DynaCLIP (88.2M params) achieves state-of-the-art physics probing (R^2=0.553/0.378/0.652 for mass/friction/restitution), outperforming DINOv2-L/14 (304M) by +26\%/+29\%/+8\%, and is the only model with positive mass R^2 on the invisible physics test (0.300 vs.\  -0.018 for all baselines).

On LIBERO-10, DynaCLIP achieves the highest average success rate (60.4\%) across 10 baselines, outperforming DINOv2 (+8.9pp), CLIP (+13.5pp), and VC-1 (+29.0pp). The advantage concentrates on physics-intensive multi-object tasks where DynaCLIP leads by 2.3...--3.3....

On DROID-100 real-world robot data, we reveal that offline action-prediction metrics are anti-correlated (rho=-0.82) with closed-loop manipulation success across 10 backbones---R3M and Theia rank highest offline but lowest on LIBERO-10, while DynaCLIP shows the opposite pattern. On CALVIN offline BC, DynaCLIP achieves the highest cosine similarity (0.479) and tied-best L1 error while also leading LIBERO-10, showing physics-aware features excel in simulated settings for both offline and online evaluation. Together, these findings expose a domain-dependent disconnect between offline and online metrics that motivates physics-grounded evaluation protocols.
On language-grounded physics queries, DynaCLIP achieves the highest Spearman rank correlation across all three physical properties (mass rho=0.647, friction rho=0.640, restitution rho=0.810), demonstrating that physics-aware pre-training enables meaningful physical reasoning from natural-language prompts.

On ManiSkill3, DynaCLIP produces the most physics-robust policies (generalization ratio 1.02... vs.\ 0.76... for CLIP), demonstrating that physics-aware features enable zero-degradation performance under physics variation.

Comprehensive ablations over 16 variants confirm that DINOv2 initialization (-97\% without), category-grounded priors (+53\% over random), and physics-contrastive training signals are each individually essential. We hope this work opens a new direction at the intersection of visual representation learning and physical scene understanding.

## Training Convergence and Hyperparameter Sensitivity


## Learning rate sensitivity.
 We swept backbone learning rates in \10^-6, 5 ... 10^-6, 10^-5, 5 ... 10^-5, 10^-4\ for 5K steps. The optimal rate (10^-5) balances physics learning and DINOv2 feature preservation. Rates  5 ... 10^-5 cause catastrophic forgetting of category structure (category accuracy drops below 0.5); rates  10^-6 produce negligible physics improvement (Delta R^2 < 0.01 on mass). The projection head learning rate (10^-3) is less sensitive, with equivalent results across 10^-4 to 10^-2.


## Temperature dynamics.
 The learnable temperature tau (initialized at 0.07) converges to  0.04 by 10K steps and remains stable thereafter. The temperature schedule _s = 0.1 for soft label construction was selected to produce well-spread soft targets; lower values (_s = 0.01) produce near-binary labels that mimic hard InfoNCE, while higher values (_s = 1.0) produce near-uniform labels that provide weak gradients.


## Batch size.
 We train with effective batch size 1,280 (128 per GPU ... 5 GPUs ... 2 gradient accumulation). Reducing to 640 degrades mass R^2 by 4\%; reducing to 320 degrades by 11\%. Larger contrastive batches provide more informative negative pairs, consistent with findings in SimCLR and MoCo.

## Analytical Physics Engine Details


Our physics engine computes trajectories analytically for five standardized diagnostic actions applied to each object:


      * Horizontal push (F = 10 N for 0.1s): Tests mass-dependent acceleration a = F/m and friction-dependent deceleration a_f = mu g.
      * Vertical push (F = 20 N upward for 0.05s): Tests mass via ballistic trajectory height h = v_0^2 / 2g where v_0 = Ft/m.
      * Grasp-lift-release (lift to h=0.5m, release): Tests restitution via bounce height h_1 = epsilon^2 h and mass via gravitational energy.
      * Lateral flick (F = 5 N for 0.02s): Tests sliding behavior under low impulse, sensitive to friction coefficient.
      * Slow press-down (F = 2 N sustained): Tests static equilibrium, producing distinct signatures for heavy vs.\ light objects.


Each action produces a 50-step trajectory at 20 Hz with 13-dimensional state vectors: position (3), orientation quaternion (4), linear velocity (3), angular velocity (3). The resulting 5...50...13 = 3,250 dimensional trajectory fingerprint captures how each object responds to standardized forces.

## Extended Training Analysis


Extending training from 30K to 100K steps improves clustering (NMI: 0.435 -> 0.517, ARI: 0.335 -> 0.480) while slightly degrading linear probing (R^2: 0.550 -> 0.536 mass). We attribute this to representation compression: longer training tightens within-material clusters but smooths fine-grained physics variations exploited by linear probes. This suggests 30K steps is optimal for downstream probing, while 100K steps may be preferred for unsupervised clustering.
